2 Choose CEFR level

CEFR Levels indicate proficiency from beginner (A1) to proficient (C2).

Words are ranked by frequency. Lower levels cover the most common words needed for basic comprehension.

3 Cards per Lemma

Lemma = the base/dictionary form of a word. For example, "correr" is the lemma for "corre", "corrí", "corriendo"; "casa" is the lemma for "casas".

1 = See only one card per lemma, skipping conjugations and declensions (e.g., "run" but not "runs", "ran", "running"). This reduces redundancy and focuses on learning root vocabulary.

1+ = See all forms including conjugations and declensions (e.g., "run", "runs", "ran", "running"), which helps with recognition of different word forms in context.

4 Exclude Cognates

Transparent cognates are words that look and mean essentially the same thing in both languages (e.g., "animal", "chocolate", "hotel").

Include = Show all words, including cognates.

Exclude = Skip transparent cognates to focus on words that require more effort to learn.

Cognate Detection Rules

A word is marked as a transparent cognate if it matches any of these patterns when comparing the Spanish word to its English translation:

Suffix Transformations

Spanish suffixes that map to English equivalents:

-ción → -tion
información → information nación → nation educación → education
-ista → -ist
artista → artist pianista → pianist terrorista → terrorist
-dad → -ty
universidad → university realidad → reality identidad → identity
-mente → -ly
normalmente → normally exactamente → exactly finalmente → finally
-oso/-osa → -ous
famoso → famous nervioso → nervous deliciosa → delicious
-ar → -ate
celebrar → celebrate separar → separate
-ado/-ada → -ated / -ed
activado → activated preparado → prepared

Phonetic Matching

English digraphs are normalized to Spanish equivalents before comparing:

ph → f, th → t
euforia → euphoria farmacia → pharmacy teatro → theater

Why Exclude Cognates?

Transparent cognates are "free" vocabulary - you likely already recognize them from English. Excluding them lets you focus study time on words that actually require memorization.

What is this?

This app teaches you Spanish vocabulary from Bad Bunny's lyrics, starting with the most common words and working toward the least common.

Why frequency order?

Language follows a power law: a small number of words make up the vast majority of speech. By learning the most frequent words first, you understand more lyrics faster.

How are percentages calculated?

The coverage percentage tells you what fraction of all words in the lyrics you'd recognize. For example, at 70% coverage you know enough words to understand roughly 70% of the words in any song. The remaining 30% are rarer words that appear less often.

How does it work?

Each word is ranked by how many times it appears across the entire discography. The app groups these into sets of 25. You work through sets in order, and each card shows the word with real lyric examples from songs where it appears.

The progress bar tracks your coverage based on the frequency of words you've learned — learning a common word contributes more to your coverage than a rare one.
